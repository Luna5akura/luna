Presentation Outline: Tongwei's Strategic Dilemma

Part 3: The External Context - Why a Decision is Necessary

(Slide 1: Introduction - Setting the Stage)

Title: Understanding the Photovoltaic Industry's Dynamic Landscape

Objective: To analyze the external forces and competitive pressures compelling Tongwei to re-evaluate its successful specialization strategy.

Agenda for Part 3:

Macro-Environmental Factors (PEST Analysis)

Competitive Environment (Porter’s Five Forces)

Key Takeaways from the Environment

(Slide 2: Macro-Environment - Key Drivers & Trends)

Political/Policy:

Tailwind: China's "Dual-Carbon" goals create massive, sustained demand for solar.

Risk: Industry is policy-dependent; changes in subsidies or tariffs can create volatility.

Economic:

Core Challenge: Extreme price volatility, especially in polysilicon. (Mention H1 2025 losses due to weak prices).

Trend: Industry consolidation is accelerating, especially in the module segment.

Technological:

Key Shift: Rapid transition from P-type to N-type cells (TOPCon, HJT).

Implication: Requires constant, heavy R&D investment to maintain a competitive edge.

(Slide 3: Competitive Environment - Porter’s Five Forces)

Rivalry Among Competitors: (STRONG)

Hyper-competitive market with intense price wars.

Dominated by large, increasingly integrated players.

Bargaining Power of Buyers: (INCREASING - CRITICAL THREAT!)

The single biggest driver for this case: Module manufacturers (Tongwei's main customers) are backward-integrating to produce their own cells.

This directly squeezes Tongwei's core business and market access.

Bargaining Power of Suppliers: (LOW to MODERATE)

Tongwei has a strong position as a major polysilicon producer, giving it leverage.

Threat of New Entrants: (MODERATE)

High growth attracts new players, but massive capital requirements and technology barriers are significant hurdles.

Threat of Substitutes: (LOW to MODERATE)

Solar's cost-effectiveness is a major advantage over other renewables like wind.

(Slide 4: Summary of External Pressures)

The market is growing, but it is also unstable and intensely competitive.

The primary strategic threat is not from direct competitors in the cell market, but from customers becoming competitors.

Standing still (specialization) is a high-risk strategy because the ground is shifting. This necessitates a strategic response.

Part 2: The Core Decision - Tongwei's Strategic Options

(Slide 5: Introduction - The Three Paths Forward)

Title: The Strategic Choice: Specialization vs. Integration

Core Question: Based on the environmental analysis, how should Tongwei position itself for future growth and stability?

The Three Options:

Full Supply Chain Integration

Partial Integration (Cell-Module Focus)

Continued Specialization

(Slide 6: Option 1 - Full Supply Chain Integration)

Description: An aggressive strategy to control the entire value chain from polysilicon to wafers, cells, and modules.

Pros / Arguments For:

Maximizes cost control and operational efficiency.

Provides a natural hedge against price volatility in any single segment.

Guarantees a sales channel for its silicon and cell output, avoiding the "customer competition" problem.

Cons / Risks:

Requires enormous capital investment and long payback periods.

High operational complexity; requires expertise in multiple different business segments.

Exposes the company to the low-margin, highly competitive module business.

(Slide 7: Option 2 - Partial Supply Chain Integration (Cell-Module Focus))

Description: A focused, defensive strategy to extend into the adjacent module segment.

Pros / Arguments For:

Directly addresses the primary threat of module makers self-producing cells.

Creates synergy for technological innovation (co-developing N-type cells and modules).

More manageable investment and risk compared to full integration.

Cons / Risks:

Creates direct competition with existing cell customers, potentially damaging relationships.

Still requires significant capital and new capabilities (e.g., branding, distribution).

Does not solve the upstream volatility risk from the polysilicon segment.

(Slide 8: Option 3 - Continued Specialization)

Description: Maintain the status quo by focusing on being the world's leader in polysilicon and solar cells.

Pros / Arguments For:

Leverages Tongwei's existing core competencies and market leadership.

Avoids massive new investments and the associated financial risks.

Allows for concentrated R&D to maintain a technological edge in its chosen segments.

Cons / Risks:

Fails to address the critical threat of customers becoming self-sufficient.

High risk of market share erosion and being "designed out" of the supply chain.

Leaves the company highly vulnerable to cyclical downturns in its specific segments.

(Slide 9: Conclusion & Recommendation Discussion)

Summary of Dilemma: Tongwei must choose between defending its profitable, specialized past and embracing a riskier, integrated future that aligns with the industry's direction.

Key Trade-offs:

Risk vs. Control: Specialization is less financially risky in the short term but offers little control over long-term market threats. Integration offers control but with immense financial exposure.

Open Question for Discussion:

"Which strategic path offers Tongwei the best balance of risk and reward for long-term, sustainable leadership in the PV industry?"
